## 📝 Quick Summary
This paper introduces **XLM-RoBERTa (XLM-R)**, a massive Transformer-based language model trained on 2.5 terabytes of filtered CommonCrawl data across 100 different languages. Unlike previous models that required explicit language tags or translation dictionaries, XLM-R learns a shared mathematical representation of human language. 

## 🎯 Key Findings & Metrics
* **Core Task Tested:** The paper heavily benchmarks the model on **XNLI (Cross-lingual Natural Language Inference)**, which is the exact task of determining if a premise in one language entails a hypothesis in another.
* **Main Result:** XLM-R completely shattered previous records, outperforming multilingual BERT (mBERT) by +14.6% average accuracy on the XNLI benchmark. It proved that scaling up unsupervised multilingual training creates models capable of deep logical reasoning across language barriers.
* **Why it works:** By sharing a massive vocabulary of 250k subword tokens across 100 languages, the model naturally aligns concepts regardless of the input language.

## 🔗 Relevance to My Thesis
This paper is the primary citation for Phase 3 of my validation pipeline (Summary Verification). 
Because my ground truth from the Open Web Index is in Dutch (e.g., Doornroosje's HTML) and the LLM's generated summary is in English, traditional text-matching completely fails. Furthermore, cosine similarity cannot catch factual contradictions (like adding the word "NOT"). 

By utilizing an XLM-RoBERTa model fine-tuned on XNLI, my Python script can perform direct Cross-Lingual Natural Language Inference. 
1. **Premise:** The raw Dutch HTML from the Parquet files.
2. **Hypothesis:** The English summary generated by the newsletter.
XLM-R will output a mathematical probability of `Entailment`, `Contradiction`, or `Neutral`, allowing me to deterministically score the factual truth of the LLM's summary without needing a translation API or relying on an "LLM-as-a-judge".
